Casual Effect Estimation

Causal effect is different from associative relationship. In reality, we are usually interested in how an action matters. For example, it is obvious that a high advertisement payment appeals to consumers, which is associative relationship. However, how to allocate your advertisement money, like focusing on young people, will get the best effect? It requires the causal effect.

Shallow men believe in luck or in circumstance. Strong men believe in cause and effect.

― Ralph Waldo Emerson

Prerequisites

Structural Causal Model

Like statistical learning, we’d better use a concise “language” to tell the causal effect. And here is the structural causal model (SCM). A SCM can always be drawn within a Directed Acyclic Graph (DAG). One reason is that the relationship doesn’t have feedback effect, with which SCM and DAG are consistent.

Fig1: A Simple SCM

In Fig1, it shows a simple causal relationship that XX affects YY, and XX is the cause. The causal effect can be written as,

Y←fX(X)Y←fX(X)

In general, XX is the treatment binary variable to be 0 or 1. Therefore, the causal effect is,

τ=YX=1−YX=0(1)(1)τ=YX=1−YX=0

Counterfactual

Here is a representative question that “how much would I earn if I went to the university?”. For an individual, he can only choose one option without foreseeing the future situation. Say, our minds cannot jump to the other parallel universe like Doctor Strange to observe his salary. Thus, we call it as counterfactual.

Fig2: Counterfactual

It leads a severe problem that we cannot estimate the treatment effect (causal effect) for one specific individual because the equation 1 requires we to analyze some variations in different worlds.

Potential Outcomes Model

Potential Outcomes Model combines the treatment, potential outcomes, observable outcome and treatment effect into one equation, which is,

Yi=XiY1i+(1−Xi)Y0i(2)(2)Yi=XiYi1+(1−Xi)Yi0

,where XiXi is the treatment. Y1iYi1 denotes the outcome after treatment while Y0iYi0 doesn’t. Therefore, YiYi is the final outcome observed. That is, if a treatment is taken, we will get Yi=Y1iYi=Yi1.

Confounding Variables

Some variables may affect treatment and outcomes simultaneously. One DAG is,

Fig3: Confounding Variables

We call ZZ blocks every path X→YX→Y. So variable ZZ makes variable XX and YY dependent. XX denotes “going to university”, YY is salary and ZZ is gender. Thus, we cannot observe the true treatment effect (X→YX→Y) by only observing XX, YY. This kind of variables called confounding variables.

For this sample, if we only treat four individuals, the ATEATE will not change because we know the outcomes before and after treatment.

ATU & ATT & SDO

  • ATU is the shorthand of average treatment effect of the untreated.
ATU=E(Y1i−Y0i|Xi=0)ATU=E(Yi1−Yi0|Xi=0)
  • ATT is the shorthand of average treatment effect of the treated.
ATT=E(Y1i−Y0i|Xi=1)ATT=E(Yi1−Yi0|Xi=1)
  • SDO is the shorthand of simple difference in mean outcomes.
SDO=E(Y1i|Xi=1)−E(Y0i|Xi=0)SDO=E(Yi1|Xi=1)−E(Yi0|Xi=0)

In the sample, we can easily calculate,

ATU=3−14=12ATT=3−14=12SDO=34−14=12ATU=3−14=12ATT=3−14=12SDO=34−14=12

Since SDOSDO is not affected by counterfactual to be accurately computed, we wonder whether it can be used to estimate ATEATE. More importantly, we can do a simple transposition for ATE based on equation 3, (let p=p(Xi=1)p=p(Xi=1))

ATE=ATT∗p+ATU∗(1−p)=ATT+(ATU−ATT)∗(1−p)=SDO+E(Y0i|Xi=0)−E(Y0i|Xi=1)+(1−p)(ATU−ATT)⇒SDO=ATE+E(Y0i|Xi=1)−E(Y0i|Xi=0)Selection Bias+(1−p)(ATT−ATU)Heterogeneous Bias(5)(5)ATE=ATT∗p+ATU∗(1−p)=ATT+(ATU−ATT)∗(1−p)=SDO+E(Yi0|Xi=0)−E(Yi0|Xi=1)+(1−p)(ATU−ATT)⇒SDO=ATE+E(Yi0|Xi=1)−E(Yi0|Xi=0)⏟Selection Bias+(1−p)(ATT−ATU)⏟Heterogeneous Bias

So how the two terms influence the estimation?

Simply regrading it as ATEATE, then a huge effect reflects if a treatment is implemented. However, the true ATEATE is 1212. The selection bias is,

Selection Bias=0+0+1+14−0+0+0+04=12Selection Bias=0+0+1+14−0+0+0+04=12

What exactly contributes to the Selection Bias? The answer is Y0iYi0. There is a probability that treated ones and untreated ones make different choices if no treatment assigned. Say, in order to ensure my estimated ATE is not distorted due to sampling bias, I must ensure treatment assignment strategy does not yield a significant difference in the potential outcome given no treatment (Y0iYi0) of treated and untreated individuals.

Confounders are generally the main cause of selection bias.

HTE=(1−12)∗(ATT−ATU)=12∗(14−34)=−14HTE=(1−12)∗(ATT−ATU)=12∗(14−34)=−14

What exactly causes Heterogeneous Treatment Effect (HTE)? The answer is Y1iYi1. When there is no sampling bias, Y1iYi1 is the main factor that influences ATTATT and ATUATU because the group of the treated and the untreated may not be the same. In this case, economists, who are not affected by the treatment, are all in the treated. In order to ensure my estimated ATE is not distorted due to sampling bias, I must ensure that my treatment assignment strategy does not yield a significant difference in the potential outcome given treatment (Y1iYi1) of treated and untreated individuals.

Randomized Controlled Trials

How to eliminate those bias? Applying Randomized Controlled Trials is a good idea. Then, treatments are independent with potential outcomes Y1iYi1 and Y0iYi0. We will have,

E(Y1i|Xi=1)=E(Y1i|Xi=0)E(Y0i|Xi=1)=E(Y0i|Xi=0)E(Yi1|Xi=1)=E(Yi1|Xi=0)E(Yi0|Xi=1)=E(Yi0|Xi=0)τ=ATE=E(Y1i−Y0i)=E(Y1i−Y0i|Xi=0/1)=ATT=ATUτ=ATE=E(Yi1−Yi0)=E(Yi1−Yi0|Xi=0/1)=ATT=ATU

ATE under Confoundedness

If there is observable confounding variables, how to estimate ATEATE? There are two criteria to figure out the ATEATE.

Back-door Criterion

Backdoor Criterion is simply controlling confounding variables constant (or nearly equal to the constant). By doing so, we will reach a magic place where treatment and outcome are independent. Remember the Fig3, the variable ZZ is ‘invisible’ in this case. Of course, we call it as conditional independent. An efficient method is matching).

As we do above, we estimate conditional ATE (CATE),

CATE=E(Y1i−Y0i|Z)(6)(6)CATE=E(Yi1−Yi0|Z)

Then the ATEATE is,

ATE=∑CATEZip(Zi)ATE=∑CATEZip(Zi)

If there are many confounding variables, controlling all confounding variables are difficult, which is known as curse of dimensionality. Under this circumstance, we can apply propensity score matching, which compresses variables into one score and all we need to do is to control the score.

Front-door Criterion

Another efficient way is Front-door Criterion. Based on Fig3, we add MM between XX and YY,

Fig4: Front-door Criteria

MM is a mediator, then the causal effect X→YX→Y is the combination of X→MX→M and M→YM→Y. Here, we can easily control XX to control MM. As a result, the influence of ZZ is eliminated.

ATEX→Y=ATEX→M∗ATEM→YATEX→Y=ATEX→M∗ATEM→Y

Front-door criteria is a very useful way because I can even have no idea about ZZ!

Causal Regression

Based on Potential Outcomes Model, we make a transposition on equation 2,

Yi=XiY1i+(1−Xi)Y0i=Xi(Y1i−Y0i)+Y0i(7)(7)Yi=XiYi1+(1−Xi)Yi0=Xi(Yi1−Yi0)+Yi0

It reminds a simple regression that contains a binary variable. And the coefficient is the ATEATE.

Assumptions

Unconfoundedness

Xi⫫(Yi(0),Yi(1))|ZiXi⫫(Yi(0),Yi(1))|Zi

This assumption tells that if there are unobserved variables, those variables are not confounders. This assumption aims at eliminating selection bias.

Overlap

∀x∈supp(X),0<P(W=1|X=x)<1∀x∈supp(X),0<P(W=1|X=x)<1

It states that every point of the covariate space we can find both the treated and control individuals.

Disjunctive Cause Criterion

Disjunctive Cause Criterion tells a methodology to select variables to control observed confounding variables. Actually, the criterion is to control variables that satisfy backdoor criterion if there are any. An example is,

Fig5: Disjunctive Example

And based on the criterion, we should choose VV and WW because they satisfy the backdoor criterion for X→YX→Y treatment.

OtherMethods

Inverse-propensity score weighting

Rosenbaum and Rubin (1983) have shown that whenever unconfoundedness holds, it is sufficient to control for the propensity score. The propensity score serves a single-dimensional variable that summarizes how observables affect the treatment probability. Let,

e(z)=P(Xi=1|Zi=z)∵Unconfoundedness,Yi⊥Xi|e(Z)e(z)=P(Xi=1|Zi=z)∵Unconfoundedness,Yi⊥Xi|e(Z)

That is, a comparison of two people with the same propensity score, one of whom received the treatment and one who did not, should in principle adjust for confounding variables. Propensity score weighting (PSW) provides our starting point as a method to reduce the effects of confounding in observational studies. The basic idea is to weight the observations to obtain similar baseline characteristics. The following results can be shown to hold,

E(Yi(1))=E(YiXie(Zi)),E(Yi(0))=E(Yi(1−Xi)1−e(Zi))E(Yi(1))=E(YiXie(Zi)),E(Yi(0))=E(Yi(1−Xi)1−e(Zi))

Thus,

τ=ATE=E(YiXie(Zi)−Yi(1−Xi)1−e(Zi))τ=ATE=E(YiXie(Zi)−Yi(1−Xi)1−e(Zi))

Weighted OLS

Using weights on OLS,

w=Xe(Z)+1−X1−e(Z)w=Xe(Z)+1−X1−e(Z)

For observations, we apply weights to make the treated and the untreated as in the similar baseline characteristics.

Causal Tree

Another important way to estimate heterogeneous treatment effect is the Causal Tree. It is an adjusted version of decision tree. Simply speaking, decision tree is efficient at capture nonlinear relationships. Thus, after classification, those ‘similar’ samples are in the same leaf. Say, we are using decision tree to control the confounders (satisfying the backdoor criterion). In each leaf, we will get the CATECATE by equation 6.

Causal Tree introduces a new splitting method and minimization criterion called Honest. Honest applies the thought of CV, and it creates SestSest to avoid overfitting in training.
